Research

Current Interests and Work

I am interested in using stars as tools to understand planet and galactic evolution. We are in an exciting time in astronomy, where the many large-scale spectroscopic and photometric surveys of stars in our Galaxy have begun to unify and entangle the sub-fields of exoplanets, stars, and galaxies. Much of my work is representative of this shift, including my current research with Professor Elisabeth Newton at Dartmouth College, where we are age-dating stars using stellar rotation to find the duty-cycles of transient spiral arms. Stay tuned for future updates.

The Astrophysical Variance of Gaia-RVS Spectra

During my time as a Bridge scholar, I worked with Professor Melissa Ness to understand the dimensionality of chemical abundance information in the several million Gaia-RVS calcium-triplet-region spectra (expected in the third data release from the Gaia Mission) to inform applications of galactic archaeology. We fit a quadratic model of four primary sources of variability described by the stellar labels of effective temperature, surface gravity, metallacity, and alpha-enrichment to the flux of spectra from the Gaia-RVS-like ARGOS survey. We find that models capture 85% of variance in the ARGOS spectra. The largest residuals are conentrated around the calcium lines, as confirmed by a principal component analysis, marking departures from the completeness of the 1D-LTE label assumption. Using the same four-label model with the Gaia-RVS-like RAVE-on Catalog, we check to see if we can infer measured Si, O, Ca, Ni, and Al abundances and find that we can. This indicates that a four-label model is sufficient in describing chemical abundance variance for SNR < 200 in Gaia-RVS spectra.
